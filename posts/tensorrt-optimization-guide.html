TensorRT in the Real World: Lessons from Optimizing 15+ Production Models

Everything I wish I knew before spending three months squeezing latency out of detection models — precision calibration, layer fusion pitfalls, and when FP16 is lying to you.

I've been optimizing models with TensorRT for about two years now. In that time I've taken 15+ PyTorch models through the full pipeline: ONNX export, engine build, validation, and production deployment. Here's what I actually learned — not the happy-path tutorial stuff, but the things that bit me.

The Baseline Before You Touch Anything

Before you start any optimization, instrument your pipeline end-to-end and record baseline numbers you trust. Not "roughly 80ms" — exact P50/P95/P99 latencies under your production load profile. You'd be surprised how many teams skip this and then can't tell if their optimization actually helped. 

# Minimal profiling harness
import time
import numpy as np

latencies = []
for _ in range(500):  # warm-up first 50, measure next 450
    t0 = time.perf_counter()
    output = model.infer(sample_input)
    t1 = time.perf_counter()
    latencies.append((t1 - t0) * 1000)

latencies = latencies[50:]
print(f"P50: {np.percentile(latencies, 50):.2f}ms")
print(f"P95: {np.percentile(latencies, 95):.2f}ms")
print(f"P99: {np.percentile(latencies, 99):.2f}ms")

FP16: Not Always a Free Speedup

The standard advice is "enable FP16, get ~2x speedup for free." This is mostly true — on Tensor Core hardware. But I've seen cases where FP16 gives you degraded accuracy that only shows up weeks later in production, when your detection rate quietly drops 2-3% on specific vehicle classes.

The culprit is usually normalization layers. BatchNorm with small epsilon values (1e-5) can underflow in FP16. TensorRT has an option to force specific layers to FP32: 

# Force BN layers to FP32
for layer in network:
    if "batch_norm" in layer.name.lower():
        layer.precision = trt.DataType.FLOAT
        layer.set_output_type(0, trt.DataType.FLOAT)

Calibration Matters More Than You Think

INT8 calibration is where most people give up because it's finicky. The calibration dataset has to actually represent your production distribution. For traffic cameras, this means: night footage, rainy conditions, partial occlusions, wide-angle distortion. If you calibrate only on daytime highway footage, your INT8 model will fall apart at night.

Calibration is not a formality. It's a mini-training run that decides where you're allowed to throw away precision. Feed it garbage, get garbage.

Layer Fusion: Trust but Verify

TensorRT's layer fusion is aggressive and usually correct, but I've hit two production bugs caused by fused ops behaving differently than the unfused equivalents at edge cases: very large input values and NaN propagation. Enable the engine inspector and sanity-check your fusion groups: 

inspector = engine.create_engine_inspector()
print(inspector.get_engine_information(trt.LayerInformationFormat.JSON))

Takeaways

  • Measure first, optimize second. Never guess where your latency is.
  • Validate FP16/INT8 on hard examples, not just average accuracy metrics.
  • Your calibration dataset is as important as your training dataset.
  • Keep the FP32 engine around for debugging — not just the optimized one.
  • Build your validation suite before you deploy any engine to production.

TensorRT is genuinely powerful. With care, we've gotten 4-6x speedups on detection models while holding accuracy within 0.1% mAP. But it rewards patience and methodical validation over quick iteration.
